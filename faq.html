Calibration Range Trainer

FAQ for learners

This page explains the ideas behind the exercise in plain English. You do not need a background in statistics to use the trainer. The main habit is simple: give a range, choose how confident you are, then learn from whether reality landed inside or outside your range.

What is this tool for?

This tool is for practising calibrated judgement. It is not mainly a trivia quiz. You will see questions where you probably do not know the exact answer, such as a population, a year in history, a distance between cities, or a practical business-like quantity. Your job is not to guess one exact number. Your job is to give a range that honestly represents your uncertainty.

That matters because many real decisions work this way. In business, strategy, operations, project planning, and risk management, you often do not know the exact answer. You still need to make a decision. A calibrated range helps you say, “Here is what I think is plausible, here is how confident I am, and here is whether more information would be worth collecting.”

What is overconfidence bias?

Overconfidence bias is the tendency to feel more certain than the evidence really supports. In this exercise, overconfidence usually shows up as ranges that are too narrow. For example, someone might say they are 90% confident that a value is between 40 and 50, when the true value is actually 73. The problem is not that they were wrong once. The problem is that their stated confidence did not match how often their ranges actually contained the truth.

Most people are not overconfident because they are careless. They are overconfident because the first answer that comes to mind feels more solid than it is. We notice reasons our answer could be right, and we often underweight reasons it could be wrong. This tool creates quick, repeated feedback so you can feel that pattern and adjust.

What is a confidence interval in this exercise?

In this exercise, a confidence interval is a lower bound and an upper bound, plus a confidence level. If you choose 80% confidence, you are saying something like: “For estimates like this, I expect the true answer to fall inside my range about 8 times out of 10.”

This is different from saying, “I hope the answer is inside my range,” or “this is my best guess.” A confidence interval is a promise about your uncertainty. If you make many 90% intervals, roughly 9 out of 10 should contain the truth. If only 5 out of 10 contain the truth, your 90% ranges are too narrow. If 10 out of 10 contain the truth but the ranges are enormous, they may be safe but not very useful.

Why do I choose a confidence level?

Choosing a confidence level makes you decide how strongly you want to stand behind your range. A high-confidence range, such as 90%, should usually be wider because you are asking it to catch the truth most of the time. A lower-confidence range, such as 50% or 60%, can be narrower because you are allowing more misses.

This is useful because real decisions do not always need the same level of certainty. Sometimes you need a conservative range because being wrong would be expensive. Other times, a narrower lower-confidence range is enough to decide whether an idea is worth exploring. The point is to match the range to the confidence claim, rather than using the same kind of range for every situation.

What is a good range?

A good range is wide enough to match what you really know, but narrow enough to be useful. The widest possible range is not automatically good. For example, if asked for the population of a country, saying “between 0 and 1 billion” will probably contain the answer, but it does not help anyone make a decision.

A useful range makes uncertainty visible. If your range is too narrow, you are pretending to know more than you do. If your range is too wide, you may be avoiding the judgement task. The skill is to find the range that sits near the edge of your understanding: honest, but still informative.

What is the equivalence bet?

The equivalence bet is a way to test whether your range and confidence level really match. Imagine two bets with the same prize. In Bet A, you win if the true answer is inside your range. In Bet B, you win a lottery with the same probability as your selected confidence. If you selected 80% confidence, Bet B is an 80% lottery.

Now ask yourself which bet you would rather take. If Bet A feels clearly safer than the lottery, your range may be too wide. You may have padded it so much that it no longer says much. If Bet B feels safer, your range may be too narrow. You may be claiming more certainty than you really have. Adjust the range until the two bets feel close. That “close call” feeling is useful: it means your stated confidence and your range are starting to line up.

How is my simple score calculated?

The simple score is deliberately easy. For each question, you get 1 point if the true answer falls inside your range. You get 0 points if it falls outside your range. A round has 5 questions, so the simple score is out of 5.

A high simple score is good, but it is not the whole story. If you get 5 out of 5 by making every range extremely wide, you have contained the answers but you may not have learned much about useful uncertainty. In a business setting, a range that is always huge may not reduce uncertainty enough to support a decision. That is why the app also shows interval score and calibration charts.

How is the interval score calculated?

The interval score rewards useful uncertainty. You do not need to memorise the formula to use the tool. The basic idea is this: a correct range is better when it is narrower, because it gave more information. But a range that misses the truth is penalised, especially if you said you were highly confident.

For example, suppose two people both miss the true answer by the same amount. One person said they were 50% confident, and the other said they were 90% confident. The 90% miss is more serious because the person made a stronger claim. The interval score captures that difference. Lower interval scores are better.

What does the calibration progress chart show?

The calibration progress chart compares two lines. The teal line shows your actual cumulative hits: it goes up by 1 whenever the true answer was inside your range, and stays flat when the answer was outside your range. The gold line shows what a perfectly calibrated result would predict from the confidence levels you selected.

Here is a small example. If your first three confidence levels are 90%, 80%, and 70%, then the perfectly calibrated line expects 0.9 + 0.8 + 0.7 = 2.4 hits after three questions. Your actual line can only move in whole points, because each question is either inside the range or outside it. Over just 5 questions, the lines will be noisy. Over repeated rounds, you want your actual results to roughly track what your confidence levels predicted.

Why should I play multiple rounds?

Five questions is enough to create feedback, but it is not enough to diagnose you perfectly. Even a well-calibrated person can have a lucky or unlucky round. Calibration is about repeated performance. If you keep choosing 80% confidence, then over many similar questions you should be right about 8 times out of 10.

Playing several rounds helps you notice your pattern. Some people discover that they are consistently too narrow. Others discover that they make very wide ranges and avoid taking an informative position. The goal is to become more aware of how your confidence behaves under uncertainty.

How can I improve my estimates?

One useful method is Fermi decomposition: break a hard question into smaller pieces that are easier to estimate. For example, instead of guessing the number of piano tuners in a city directly, estimate the number of people, the number of households, the share with pianos, how often pianos are tuned, and how many jobs a tuner can do. Each piece may still be uncertain, but the structure gives you a better starting point.

Also practise challenging your first anchor. Ask, “What would make my estimate wrong?” or “What would I believe if my first answer were too low?” For unfamiliar questions, widen your interval before submitting. A high-confidence range should often feel wider than your first instinct.

How does this apply to business?

Business decisions often depend on uncertain quantities: market size, demand, cost, delivery time, project delay, churn, conversion rate, productivity improvement, operational risk, or the value of a new product. You may not know the exact number, but you can still estimate a range and attach a confidence level.

Once uncertainty is expressed as a range, it becomes easier to discuss. You can ask whether the uncertainty is big enough to affect the decision. You can ask what information would narrow the range. You can also decide whether measuring something is worth the cost. This is the practical point behind calibrated estimation: better uncertainty estimates lead to better decisions.

What should I remember after the workshop?

Remember the habit more than the scores. When you face an uncertain quantity, do not only give a single-point guess. Give a range. Attach a confidence level. Test the range with the equivalence bet. Ask why you might be wrong. Then update from feedback.

Calibration is not about becoming perfectly accurate overnight. It is about becoming more honest, more disciplined, and more useful when you reason under uncertainty. That skill travels well from quiz questions to business judgement.
